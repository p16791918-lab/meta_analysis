# SABCS Submission — Conference Abstract

**Title:**
Age-Stratified Racial Disparities in Breast Cancer Incidence:
A Systematic Review and Preliminary Meta-Analysis

**Presenting Author:** J. Park
**Institution:** Pusan National University (PNU), Busan, Republic of Korea
**Session Category:** Disparities / Epidemiology

---

**Background:**
Racial disparities in breast cancer incidence are well-documented, yet their
direction and magnitude differ substantially across age groups, cancer subtypes,
and geographic settings. Existing reviews rarely quantify these patterns in a
unified framework. We conducted a systematic review and preliminary
meta-analysis to characterize Black–White differences in breast cancer
incidence rates across age strata.

**Methods:**
PubMed was searched from January 2015 to April 2025 using MeSH terms for
breast neoplasms, racial groups, and incidence, restricted to English-language,
human studies with abstracts. Of 649 identified records, 25 were screened in
full; 16 met inclusion criteria requiring multiracial comparisons with
quantitative incidence data. Where studies reported incidence rate ratios (IRR)
with 95% confidence intervals (CIs), a random-effects meta-analysis was
performed using the DerSimonian–Laird method with Black versus White women as
the primary comparison.

**Results:**
Five effect sizes from two independent studies were eligible for pooling. The
pooled IRR for Black versus White women was 1.32 (95% CI: 1.11–1.57),
indicating 32% higher overall risk in Black women. Substantial heterogeneity
was observed (I² = 99.2%, p < 0.001), driven largely by an age-dependent
crossover effect: Black women had markedly higher incidence at ages 20–29
(IRR = 1.53, 95% CI: 1.43–1.65) and 30–39 years (IRR = 1.15, 95% CI:
1.12–1.18), but lower incidence at ages 40–49 (IRR = 0.96, 95% CI: 0.94–0.97)
relative to White women (SEER-17, 2000–2019; N = 217,815). Among
registry-based studies, annual incidence growth in women ≥65 years was 7–11
times faster in Black, Hispanic, and Asian/Pacific Islander women compared
with White women (US Cancer Statistics, 2001–2019; N = 2,278,611).

**Conclusions:**
Black women face a substantially higher breast cancer incidence before age 40,
with the racial gap narrowing and reversing in midlife. These age-specific
patterns underscore the inadequacy of a single pooled estimate and support
earlier screening initiation for Black women. Future analyses incorporating
Embase and CINAHL with incidence-only outcomes are warranted to reduce
heterogeneity and strengthen these preliminary findings.
